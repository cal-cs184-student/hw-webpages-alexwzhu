Overview

In this assignment, I built a real-time cloth simulator based on a mass-spring model. The cloth is represented as a 2D grid of point masses connected by three types of springs (structural, shearing, and bending), and it is driven forward in time using Verlet integration with Provot's length-correction step. On top of the core simulator, I added collision response against spheres and planes, efficient self-collision via spatial hashing, and a set of GLSL shaders implementing diffuse, Blinn-Phong, texture, bump, displacement, and environment-mapped mirror materials.

The most interesting takeaway for me was seeing how emergent cloth behavior comes out of very local rules: three very simple Hookean spring types plus a cheap position post-processing step give convincing drape and folding. Tuning parameters like ks, density, and damping really highlighted the trade-offs between stiffness, inertia, and stability.

Part 1: Masses and springs

In Part 1, I implemented Cloth::buildGrid, which lays out the point masses in a 2D grid and then connects them with the three spring types.

Point masses. I iterate over the grid in row-major order (outer loop over the height index j, inner loop over the width index i) so that point (i, j) lands at index j * num_width_points + i. For each mass I compute evenly spaced coordinates in [0, width] × [0, height]. If the cloth orientation is HORIZONTAL, the grid lies in the XZ plane with y = 1; otherwise it lies in the XY plane with a small random z offset sampled uniformly from [-1/1000, 1/1000], which gives the vertical cloth a slight natural wrinkle so it doesn't fall as a flat sheet. I then check the cloth's pinned list to mark masses whose (i, j) matches.

Part 2: Simulation via numerical integration

In Part 2, I implemented Cloth::simulate, which advances the cloth one timestep.

Accumulating forces. Each simulation step I reset every point mass's force to the total external force. External force is computed from Newton's second law: for each acceleration in external_accelerations I add mass * a to a running sum, and then assign this to every mass's forces vector. I then iterate over all springs, skipping spring types that are disabled by ClothParameters. For each enabled spring, Hooke's law gives a magnitude of k_s * (||p_a - p_b|| - l). I multiply this by 0.2 for bending springs (which should be much weaker than structural and shearing). The force is applied in the direction between the two masses and equal-and-opposite forces are added to pm_a and pm_b.

Verlet integration. For every non-pinned point mass, I apply \[ x_{t+\Delta t} = x_t + (1 - d)(x_t - x_{t-\Delta t}) + a_t \, \Delta t^2 \] where \( d \) is the damping coefficient divided by 100 and \( a_t = F/m \). I update last_position and position accordingly. Pinned masses are left untouched.

Provot length correction. After integration (and after collision handling), I scan springs and clamp any that have stretched beyond 1.10 * rest_length. Given the over-stretch, I push the two endpoints back along the spring direction symmetrically (50/50). If one endpoint is pinned, the other takes the full correction; if both are pinned, the spring is skipped.

Effect of ks. Increasing ks makes the cloth behave as a much stiffer sheet. Very low ks lets the cloth stretch aggressively under its own weight and the resulting drape is exaggerated.

Effect of density. Increasing density scales the per-mass weight directly; under the same ks, a heavier cloth sags more because gravity forces scale linearly with mass while spring restoring forces do not.

Effect of damping. Very low damping lets the cloth oscillate for a long time - it swings back and forth like an underdamped spring system. Very high damping kills velocity almost immediately and the cloth falls slowly.

Part 3: Handling collisions with other objects

After Velot moves masses, if any of them end up inside an object just move them back out.

Sphere. Given the point mass's position and the sphere's origin, I check whether the squared distance is less than radius^2. If so, I compute the tangent point as origin + r * normalize(position - origin). The correction vector is tangent - last_position. The new position is last_position + (1 - friction) * correction. This both places the point on the sphere surface and reduces the effective velocity produced by Verlet on the next step.

Plane. I detect a plane crossing by comparing the signed distances of last_position and position to the plane (using the dot product with the plane normal). If they have opposite signs, the trajectory crossed during this step. I parameterize the trajectory from last_position to position and solve for the crossing parameter t to find the tangent point on the plane. I then place a target just above the plane on the original side using the SURFACE_OFFSET constant, and set position = last_position + (1 - friction) * (target - last_position).

In Cloth::simulate, after Verlet integration, I iterate over every point mass and call collide(pm) on every collision object.

Cloth on sphere for varying ks. Low ks gives a very soft, conforming drape with many small folds around the sphere. At the default ks = 5000, the cloth takes a natural rest shape with moderate wrinkling. With very high ks the cloth becomes stiff and resists bending.

Part 4: Handling self-collisions

Brute-force pairwise self-collision would be \(O(n^2)\) in the number of point masses. To make it more controllable for a 50×50 grid, I used uniform spatial hashing.

hash_position. I partition 3D space into rectangular boxes of dimensions w = 3 * width / num_width_points, h = 3 * height / num_height_points, and t = max(w, h). For a given position, I use floor to find the integer box index along each axis, then combine the three indices into a single float using large prime-like multipliers to keep distinct boxes mapping to distinct hash keys.

build_spatial_map. Before self-collision, I clear out the old map (deleting the owned vectors), then walk every point mass and push a pointer into the vector keyed by the mass's hash.

self_collide. For a given point mass, I look up the vector of candidate point masses sharing its hash bucket. For every candidate (excluding the point mass itself), if the distance between them is strictly less than 2 * thickness, I accumulate a correction vector that would push the two masses apart exactly to 2 * thickness along the direction between them. I average the corrections over the number of colliding candidates and divide once more by simulation_steps, so that across a full frame the cumulative correction is on the order of 2 * thickness, not simulation_steps * 2 * thickness.

Progression from falling to rest. The cloth begins falling onto itself and each frame the self-collision pass pushes overlapping layers apart, so the layers remain distinct instead of interpenetrating. As the cloth continues to settle, it piles up into a coherent folded shape.

Varying ks. With a low spring constant, the cloth is much more compliant: it deforms easily under its own weight and the resulting pile has tightly packed, wrinkly folds. With a high spring constant, the springs strongly resist stretching, so the cloth keeps a more sheet-like character and folds in larger, more cylindrical rolls — there are fewer fine wrinkles and the layers stay more separated.

Varying density. Lower density makes the cloth lighter, so each layer presses down with less weight and the pile keeps clearer, rounder rolls in its rest shape. Higher density adds mass uniformly, which means more inertia during the fall and heavier compaction once the cloth lands — the layers crush down on each other and the pile becomes flatter and more chaotic.

Part 5: Shaders

A shader is a small GPU program that runs in parallel at a specific stage of the graphics pipeline. In this assignment we used two stages: the vertex shader, which runs once per vertex and is responsible for transforming positions into clip space and forwarding per-vertex attributes (normals, UVs, tangents) to the next stage, and the fragment shader, which runs once per pixel covered by a rasterized triangle and produces the final output color.

Blinn-Phong. The Blinn-Phong model combines three terms: \[ L = k_a I_a + k_d \frac{I}{r^2} \max(0, n \cdot l) + k_s \frac{I}{r^2} \max(0, n \cdot h)^p \] The ambient term is a constant that approximates indirect light and prevents shadowed regions from going pure black. The diffuse term is Lambertian shading — the surface brightness falls off with the cosine of the angle between the surface normal \(n\) and the light direction \(l\), and the intensity is attenuated by the inverse square of distance to the light. The specular term is the characteristic highlight: it uses the halfway vector \(h = (l + v)/\|l+v\|\) and the surface normal, raised to a shininess exponent \(p\) that controls how tight the highlight is. Larger \(p\) gives a small, sharp highlight; smaller \(p\) spreads it out.

Texture mapping. For texture mapping, the fragment shader simply samples u_texture_1 at the interpolated UV and outputs that color. The vertex shader forwards in_uv unchanged as v_uv, and GL interpolates it across the triangle.

Bump vs. displacement. Bump mapping and displacement mapping both use a height map \(h(u,v)\), but they differ in what they change. Bump mapping only modifies the shading normals: the geometry is untouched, so silhouettes are flat. Displacement mapping actually moves vertices along the normal by \(h(u,v) k_h\) in the vertex shader so that the silhouette is deformed as well.

For the bump fragment shader, I build a TBN matrix from the interpolated normal and tangent (with bitangent as cross(n, t)), compute local height gradients \(dU\) and \(dV\) from finite differences on the height map, form the local-space perturbed normal n_o = (-dU, -dV, 1), transform it into model space with TBN, normalize, and then run Blinn-Phong with this new normal. The displacement shader uses exactly the same fragment logic; the difference lives in the vertex shader, where each vertex is displaced by in_normal * h(in_uv) * u_height_scaling before the model and view-projection matrices are applied.

For both shaders I used the same non-default texture as the height source: I replaced texture_2.png with the checkered texture_4.png so the same image drives the bump normal perturbation and the vertex displacement.

Bump mapping on the cloth and sphere. On the cloth, bump mapping adds the illusion of fabric weave and surface wrinkles that follow the drape. On the sphere, the lighting looks visibly bumpy even though the silhouette of the sphere remains perfectly smooth.

Displacement mapping on the sphere. In contrast, the displacement shader actually moves vertices outward along the surface normal by h(uv) * u_height_scaling, so the silhouette of the sphere is no longer a perfect circle.

Comparison: bump vs. displacement. Bump mapping is cheap because it never changes any geometry — all the detail is an optical illusion produced by perturbing the normal per fragment, so it only costs a few extra texture reads. Displacement mapping gives "real" geometric detail, but it is dependent on the tessellation of the underlying mesh: you only have as much displacement resolution as you have vertices. This means displacement correctly deforms silhouettes and casts correct parallax, but it's more expensive and loses detail at low tessellation.

Sphere coarseness comparison (-o 16 -a 16 vs. -o 128 -a 128). At low tessellation, bump mapping still looks convincingly bumpy in shading but the sphere's outline is visibly polygonal. Displacement at the same tessellation looks noticeably worse: there aren't enough vertices to represent fine height features, so the bumpy detail on the sphere surface is lost and the silhouette only captures the largest height variations. At high tessellation, bump mapping looks essentially identical to the low-tessellation version in terms of shading detail. Displacement, on the other hand, shows its full resolution.

Mirror (environment mapping). In the mirror fragment shader, I compute the eye ray wo = normalize(v_position - u_cam_pos), reflect it across the surface normal using GLSL's reflect, and sample the cubemap u_texture_cubemap with the reflected direction. The result is a pure perfect-reflection surface that picks up the environment in the cubemap. On the sphere the reflection is very clean because the normals are smooth, while on the cloth the reflection gets warped along the folds.
