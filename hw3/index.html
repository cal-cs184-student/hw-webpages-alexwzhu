Overview

In this homework I built a physically-based path tracer from the ground up. The pipeline starts with generating camera rays and testing them against scene geometry (Part 1), then accelerating those intersection tests with a BVH (Part 2). From there I implemented direct lighting using both uniform hemisphere sampling and light importance sampling (Part 3), extended it to full global illumination with recursive bounces and Russian Roulette termination (Part 4), and finally added adaptive sampling to concentrate computation where it's actually needed (Part 5).

The most interesting takeaway for me was seeing how much each piece of the pipeline matters. BVH alone turned renders from minutes to milliseconds. Importance sampling over lights cut noise dramatically compared to blind hemisphere sampling at the same sample count. And Russian Roulette gave me an unbiased way to handle infinite-length paths without actually tracing them forever. It was satisfying to watch the renders go from flat normal-shaded images to fully lit scenes with color bleeding and soft shadows.

Part 1: Ray Generation and Scene Intersection

Implementation

For ray generation, I map normalized image coordinates to a point on the virtual camera sensor (on the Z=-1 plane in camera space), using hFov and vFov to determine the sensor extents. The ray goes from the camera origin through that sensor point, gets transformed to world space via the c2w matrix, and has its min_t/max_t set to the near/far clip planes. In raytrace_pixel, I generate ns_aa jittered samples per pixel using a uniform grid sampler, trace each ray, and average the results.

For triangle intersection I used the Moller-Trumbore algorithm, which solves for the ray parameter t and barycentric coordinates u, v simultaneously using cross products and determinants. If the intersection is within the ray's valid [min_t, max_t] range, I update max_t and (for the full intersect version) populate the Intersection struct with the interpolated normal, primitive pointer, and BSDF. Sphere intersection uses the standard quadratic formula, picking the closest valid root.

Part 2: Bounding Volume Hierarchy

BVH Construction Algorithm

The BVH is built recursively. At each node, I compute the bounding box of all primitives in the current range. If there are fewer than max_leaf_size primitives, I just store them in a leaf and return. Otherwise, I split the primitives into two groups and recurse on each half.

Splitting heuristic: I pick whichever axis has the largest extent in the bounding box, then compute the average centroid of all primitives along that axis and use it as the split point. Primitives with centroids below the split go left, and the rest go right. If every primitive ends up on one side (which would cause infinite recursion), I fall back to just putting half the primitives in each child.

Part 3: Direct Illumination

Uniform Hemisphere Sampling

For estimate_direct_lighting_hemisphere, I sample num_samples random directions uniformly on the hemisphere around the hit point. For each sampled direction, I cast a ray from the hit point and check if it hits something emissive. If it does, I evaluate the reflection equation: multiply the BSDF value f(w_out, w_i) by the incoming radiance and cos(theta) (the z-component of w_i in object space). Since the pdf of a uniform hemisphere sample is 1/(2*PI), I multiply the sum by 2*PI / num_samples at the end. One thing I had to be careful about was keeping track of which vectors were in object space vs world space — the BSDF expects object-space directions, but the shadow ray needs to be in world space.

Importance Sampling Lights

For estimate_direct_lighting_importance, instead of blindly sampling directions, I loop over each light in the scene and sample directions toward it. For each light, I take ns_area_light samples (or just 1 for point lights, since every sample would be identical). Each call to light->sample_L gives me the incoming radiance, the direction to the light, the distance, and the pdf. I skip samples where the light is behind the surface (checked via dot(w_i, normal) <= 0). Then I cast a shadow ray from the hit point toward the light, with max_t = distToLight - EPS_F so I only detect blockers between the surface and the light. If nothing blocks it, I evaluate the reflection equation with the sampled pdf. I average the contribution per light, then sum across all lights.

Hemisphere vs. Importance Sampling

Importance sampling produces significantly less noise than hemisphere sampling at the same sample count. The reason is straightforward: hemisphere sampling wastes most of its samples on directions that don't point toward any light source, so only a small fraction of samples actually contribute to the estimate. Importance sampling, on the other hand, directly samples directions toward each light, so every sample has a chance of contributing. This is especially visible in the soft shadow regions, where hemisphere sampling produces a lot of speckle even at higher sample counts, while importance sampling converges to smooth gradients much faster. Importance sampling also handles point lights correctly (hemisphere sampling can't hit a point light with probability > 0), and overall gives better results for the same render time.

Part 4: Global Illumination

Indirect Lighting Implementation

In at_least_one_bounce_radiance, I start by adding the direct (one-bounce) lighting if I'm accumulating bounces or if this is the last bounce. Then, if there are bounces remaining, I sample a new direction using bsdf->sample_f (which gives a cosine-weighted direction for diffuse surfaces), cast a ray in that direction, and recursively call the same function on whatever it hits. The indirect contribution gets weighted by the BSDF value, cos(theta), and divided by the pdf.

For Russian Roulette, I use a continuation probability of 0.65. On every indirect bounce after the first, I flip a coin — if it comes up "terminate," I stop and return what I have. If I continue, I divide the indirect contribution by 0.65 to keep the estimator unbiased. I always trace at least one indirect bounce (when max_ray_depth > 1) so that the first bounce isn't randomly dropped. The isAccumBounces flag controls whether I return light from all bounces up to the current depth, or only the light at exactly the m-th bounce.

I also needed to set r.depth = max_ray_depth on camera rays in raytrace_pixel, and update est_radiance_global_illumination to call at_least_one_bounce_radiance instead of just one_bounce_radiance. One thing I had to get right was the isAccumBounces=false case — when not accumulating, I zero out the direct lighting contribution before adding the recursive result, so only the m-th bounce shows through.

Part 5: Adaptive Sampling

Explanation

Adaptive sampling avoids using a fixed high sample count for every pixel. Some pixels converge quickly (e.g. a flat wall lit uniformly), while others need many more samples (e.g. shadow edges, areas with complex indirect lighting). The idea is to check whether a pixel has converged as I go, and stop early if it has.

Implementation

I modified raytrace_pixel to track running sums s1 (sum of illuminances) and s2 (sum of squared illuminances) as I trace samples. Every samplesPerBatch samples, I compute the mean and variance:

\(\mu = s_1 / n\),   \(\sigma^2 = \frac{1}{n-1}(s_2 - s_1^2/n)\)

Then I compute the convergence measure \(I = 1.96 \cdot \sigma / \sqrt{n}\). If \(I \leq \texttt{maxTolerance} \cdot \mu\), the pixel has converged at a 95% confidence level and I stop sampling. The 1.96 comes from the z-score for a 95% confidence interval. I write the actual number of samples taken to sampleCountBuffer so the rate image shows how many samples each pixel used.

One thing to be careful about is only checking convergence every samplesPerBatch samples (not every single sample), since the convergence check involves a square root and division that would add up if done per-sample.

Results (2048 max samples, samplesPerBatch=64, maxTolerance=0.05)

In the sample rate images, blue means fewer samples were needed and red means more. The flat walls and floor converge quickly (blue), while shadow boundaries, the area around the light source, and areas with complex indirect lighting require many more samples (red). This matches intuition — regions with high variance in the radiance estimate need more samples to pin down.
